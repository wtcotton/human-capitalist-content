# Trent Cotton's Voice

Source of truth for how Trent Cotton sounds. Derived from cleaned episode
transcripts of *The Human Capitalist*. Apply this when writing or reviewing any
content meant to sound like Trent the host.

## The one-line version

Trent is a **friendly antagonist** to HR's conventional wisdom — warm,
practitioner-grounded, data-backed, and allergic to hype. He challenges the
industry by swinging *with* the guest, never by lecturing the audience.

## Pick the register first

There are two registers. Choose on purpose based on the asset.

- **Spoken Trent** (podcast, video hooks, TikTok, looser LinkedIn, episode
  debriefs): loose, repetitive, full of personality — triple-taps ("I love it.
  I love it. I love it."), asides ("God love them"), personal cold-opens. This
  is where his personality lives. Some otherwise-"banned" words (e.g. "unpack")
  appear naturally here.
- **Written Trent** (blog posts, polished published copy, newsletter): the
  tightened version — no clichés, no hedge words, no pseudo-influencer tropes,
  first-person POV throughout.

Don't sand off all the personality chasing "clean." A blog can be tight; a hook
or social post must carry the spoken cadence or it stops sounding like him.

## Core voice traits (with evidence)

### 1. Friendly antagonist to industry orthodoxy
Targets lazy industry thinking, **never the guest**.
- "Let's blow up the use of the CV or the resume. I wanna add to that: can we
  actually get a job description and not a job advertisement?"
- "This is a time for HR to step into this and lead this charge. And if you're
  not up for it, then my encouragement is to step out of the industry."
- "I love how you call out this misunderstanding that something has to be binary."

### 2. Skeptical of hype, vendors, and platforms (incl. LinkedIn)
- "One of the things I like about LinkedIn — not their algorithm, but I do love
  the connections."
- "LinkedIn still trying to be relevant. God love them." / "the big blue monster"
- "Of course you did a survey on AI because you want us all to use your AI and
  your inflated numbers on your AI."
- "There's a lot of sizzle, but not a lot of steak." (Tim Sackett callback)
- On hype: "it's agentic, agentic, agentic, but practitioners go — we're just
  comfortable with automation."

### 3. Practitioner-grounded and data-backed
- "I probably have data to prove it, right?"
- Frameworks he reaches for: the **three-legged stool** (assessments), **"grunt
  work"** (automatable tasks), **scorecard vs. commercial** (job descriptions).
- Cites real signals: workforce reports, KPIs, attrition numbers.

### 4. Lived-experience storytelling as proof
Rarely makes an abstract point without a personal anecdote.
- "I started out as a teller in a bank and fell in love with banking… it
  completely altered my career trajectory."
- The mom story: "Debbie, you have an iPhone. Do you ever talk to Siri? She
  goes, yeah. I said — that's AI."
- The CFO friend who dumped company financials into ChatGPT unredacted.

### 5. Self-aware about his own intensity (humanizing)
- "For a lot of my career, people misunderstood my passion for aggression… I've
  had to learn how to temper it."
- "Whenever I'm charging up a hill, I'm ready to die on it."
- "My grandmother always said my face comes with subtitles."

### 6. Depth over surface — and he names it
- "These are the conversations that need to be had rather than just keeping it
  surface level."
- "10 minutes is not enough to pull this out." / "We just cracked the surface."
- "I love the back and forth."

### 7. Relational warmth and community
- Names and elevates people generously: "Love me some Josh Jones," shout-outs to
  Shallie ("the OG"), Tim Sackett.
- Curiosity as a value: "two data people chasing a rabbit."

## Rhythm & verbal signature

- **Triple-tap emphasis:** "I love it. I love it. I love it." / "Same, same,
  same." / "Absolutely. Absolutely."
- **Affirm-then-build:** agrees with the guest, then adds a sharper layer —
  "I'm gonna tack on three pieces of advice…"
- **Warm openers:** "Well, why don't you tell everyone a little bit about your
  background…"
- **Connective tissue:** "you know," "I mean," "kind of," "really, really,"
  "so let's shift gears."
- **Generational lens:** Boolean search → prompt engineering ("we had to learn
  how to talk to the computer").
- **AI optimism, practitioner-framed:** "Finally HR can get out of the process
  and get back into the people."

## Signature-phrase bank (use sparingly, don't parody)

- "Let's blow up the [resume / process]."
- "It's not binary." / "There's a happy medium."
- "Sizzle, not steak."
- "God love them." / "the big blue monster" (LinkedIn)
- "In my day job…" (firewall phrase — see below)
- "Let's shift gears."
- "I love the back and forth."
- "Charging up a hill I'm ready to die on."

## Do / Don't

**Do**
- Lead with a POV and back it with a personal story or a data point.
- Challenge the industry's defaults; invite the reader in rather than scold.
- Keep it first-person and conversational.
- Be specific and concrete (real numbers, real anecdotes, named people).
- Let optimism about AI sit next to skepticism about hype.

**Don't**
- Sound like a vendor or press release. Never position a tool/platform as the hero.
- Use hedge words ("perhaps," "it might be argued").
- Use pseudo-influencer tropes in **published** copy ("game-changer," "lean
  into," and minimize "unpack").
- Default to second person ("you should") in what should be a first-person take.
- Write generic "5 things HR leaders should know" listicle filler.

## The iCIMS firewall (hard rule)

Trent the host ≠ Trent the iCIMS employee. Day-job work is referenced only as
**"my day job"** — never as a named product, platform, or solution. The word
"iCIMS" should appear only if Trent is disclosing his affiliation as context.
In the transcripts this is done cleanly: "a post I did for my day job," "in my
day job I'm spending time with practitioners." Mirror that.
